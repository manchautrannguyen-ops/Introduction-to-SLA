Chapter 6

Second Language Development

6.0 Chapter Overview

L2 (1) is shaped by a number of identifiable processes and influences. Understanding these helps explain both the systematic patterns and the errors that characterize learner language across all linguistic sub-areas.

6.1 Common Processes and Influences

6.1.1 Cross-Linguistic Influence (Transfer)

  • Defined as the influence resulting from similarities and differences between the target language and any previously acquired language (Odlin, 1989).
  • Affects all linguistic subsystems: phonology, morphology, syntax, semantics, pragmatics, and orthography.
  • Now commonly called Cross-Linguistic Influence (CLI), which can affect both comprehension and production.
  • Two directions:
  • Positive transfer: L1 similarity facilitates L2 acquisition (e.g., learners whose L1 has articles acquire English articles faster).
  • Negative transfer ((2)): L1 differences cause L2 errors (e.g., Finnish speakers omitting prepositions in English due to L1 absence of similar prepositions).
  • Typologically similar languages lead to more transfer (Ringbom, 1987).
  • Transfer is influenced by: L1–L2 similarity, learner's proficiency level, literacy in L1.
  • Not fully predictable — learners sometimes avoid transferring even structures that would work correctly in the L2.

6.1.2 Overgeneralization

  • Learners over-apply target language rules beyond their proper scope.
  • Example: "I eated" (applying regular past -ed to an irregular verb).
  • A specific type — overregularization — is particularly common in verb (3).
  • Proves learners have internalized rules and are actively using them, not merely imitating input.
  • Parallels overgeneralization found in L1 acquisition (see Chapter 2).

6.1.3 Markedness

  • Markedness = the idea that some linguistic features are more natural/universal (unmarked) while others are rarer or more complex (marked).
  • Example: voiceless (4) (p, t, k) = unmarked; voiced stops (b, d, g) = more marked.
  • Markedness Differential Hypothesis (MDH) (Eckman, 1977): difficulty in L2 is predictable not just by difference from L1, but by how marked the target feature is relative to the L1.
  • Features that differ from L1 AND are more marked → difficult.
  • Features that differ from L1 but are NOT more marked → not necessarily difficult.
  • General principle: if a language has a marked structure, it will also have the unmarked equivalent. Learners acquire unmarked features more easily than marked ones.

6.1.4 Developmental Sequences

  • L2 learners follow fixed, predictable stages of development regardless of L1 background, though L1 can exert some influence.
  • Example: English negation development in Spanish L1 learners (Cancino et al., 1978):
  • "No V" (No go)
  • "Don't V" (unanalyzed)
  • Aux-neg (he didn't go)
  • Similar patterns found in German L2 negation development.
  • Suggests universal developmental constraints are at work beyond just L1 (5).

6.1.5 Formulaic Sequences

  • Formulaic sequences = prefabricated chunks stored and retrieved as whole units (Wray, 2000): "I don't know," "Wait a minute," "What's that?"
  • Serve as building blocks in L2 development; allow learners to express complex ideas before mastering the underlying grammar.
  • Help reduce cognitive processing (6), freeing attention for other aspects of language learning.
  • Increased use of formulaic language is linked to improved L2 fluency (Boers et al., 2006; McGuire, 2009).
  • Adult L2 learners of Swedish used formulaic expressions containing structures beyond their current mastery — suggesting formulas help learners bootstrap into more complex language (Bolander, 1989).
  • The Lexical Approach (Lewis, 1997) builds on this insight by teaching vocabulary, collocations, and fixed expressions rather than grammar rules.

6.1.6 Fossilization

  • Fossilization (Selinker, 1972): certain non-native-like forms become permanently fixed in a learner's (7) despite instruction and exposure.
  • Example: Mila omitting English articles ("You can use black cup") because articles are absent in her native Bosnian — even after years in the US.
  • Common fossilized features: non-target-like pronunciation (e.g., "th" → "t" or "z"), non-count noun pluralization ("homeworks").
  • Not all researchers accept permanent fossilization — Long (2003) suggests the term "stabilization" as an alternative, since learners may eventually correct fossilized forms.

6.1.7 Avoidance

  • Learners sometimes avoid structures perceived as difficult rather than risk producing errors.
  • Example: Xia Mei avoiding English relative clauses, splitting complex sentences into two simpler ones.
  • A key limitation of Error Analysis — errors don't reveal what learners are systematically avoiding.
  • Schachter (1974): Japanese and Chinese learners made fewer relative (8) errors than Arabic/Farsi learners, but only because they used them far less often — their L1 structures relative clauses very differently from English.

6.2 L2 Development Across Linguistic Sub-Areas

6.2.1 Phonology

  • Pronunciation is the most difficult area to master and the most obvious indicator of non-native competence ("foreign accent").
  • L1 acts as a "phonological (9)" (Trubetzkoy) — new sounds are filtered through existing L1 categories.
  • Key distinction: perception vs production — L2 perception is generally more accurate than production, though exceptions exist (some learners produce sounds they cannot reliably perceive).
  • Speech Learning Model (SLM) (Flege, 1995): sounds similar to L1 sounds are harder to acquire than entirely new sounds, because learners map L2 sounds onto existing L1 categories. New sounds trigger creation of new phonetic categories.
  • Markedness also applies to phonology: unmarked sounds (e.g., voiceless stops word-finally) easier to acquire than marked equivalents (e.g., voiced stops word-finally).
  • L2 learner strategies when encountering difficult sounds: epenthesis (inserting a vowel), deletion, or feature change (devoicing).

6.2.2 Vocabulary

  • Vocabulary is essential: "Without vocabulary nothing can be conveyed" (Wilkins, 1972).
  • Challenges include: segmenting continuous speech into words, the vast size of language vocabulary (~54,000 word families in English; ~20,000 known by university graduates).
  • Practical targets:
  • 1,000 most frequent words ≈ 90% of spoken conversations.
  • ~2,000 word families needed for 80% text comprehension.
  • ~8,000–9,000 word families needed for 98% text comprehension (required for adequate reading).
  • Learning a new word from reading context requires knowing ~95% of surrounding words (Nation, 2001).
  • Receptive (10) (recognizing words) develops before productive knowledge (actively using words); productive learning takes longer and decays faster.
  • Knowing a word requires: meaning, written and spoken form, grammatical behavior, collocations, register, associations, and frequency (Nation, 1990) — not all acquired simultaneously.
  • First step in L2 word acquisition: mapping L2 words to L1 translation equivalents; but idiomatic use requires building L2-specific semantic structures (Jiang, 2004).
  • Cognates (words sharing form/meaning across languages, e.g., "chair"/"chaise") are acquired faster and recognized more quickly.
  • Factors delaying word acquisition: difficult pronunciation, length, grammatical/morphological complexity, multiple or metaphorical meanings.

6.2.3 Grammatical Development

6.2.3.1 Morphology
  • Following Brown's (1973) L1 morpheme study (Chapter 2), researchers found L2 learners from different L1 backgrounds acquire English grammatical morphemes in a similar — but not identical — order to L1 acquirers.
  • L2 English morpheme acquisition order (Krashen, 1977):
  • -ing, plural -s, copula "be"
  • Auxiliary "be," a/the
  • Irregular past
  • Regular past -ed, 3rd person -s, possessive -s
  • Goldschneider & DeKeyser's (2001) meta-analysis confirmed this order, attributing it to: perceptual (11), semantic complexity, morphophonological regularity, syntactic category, and frequency.
  • Important caution: accuracy order across learners may not equal acquisition order within a single learner over time.
6.2.3.2 Syntax
  • Multidimensional Model (Meisel, Clahsen & Pienemann, 1981): from studying 45 immigrant workers learning German, five fixed stages of word-order development were identified:
StageFeatureExample
1Canonical SVO order"Die Kinder spielen mit dem Ball."
2Adverb fronting (no inversion)"Da Kinder spielen."
3Verb/particle separation"Alle kinder muss die pause machen."
4Inversion"Dann hat sie wieder die knoch gebringt."
5Verb-final subordinate clause"Er sagte, dass er nach hause kommt."
  • Processability (12) (Pienemann, 1998): stages emerge in order because each requires increasingly greater cognitive processing load.
  • Teachability Hypothesis (Pienemann, 1988): instruction only succeeds when a learner is cognitively ready — teaching a structure one stage beyond the learner's current level works; teaching across multiple stages does not.
6.2.3.3 English L2 Question Development
  • Five developmental (13) identified (Johnston, 1985; Pienemann et al., 1988) across Vietnamese and Polish L1 learners:
StageExample
1Single words/formulae: "Yes?"
2SVO + rising intonation: "You like Chinese food?"
3Do-fronting: "Do he work?"
4aYes/no inversion: "Have you car?"
4bWh + copula inversion: "Where is my purse?"
5Auxiliary inversion: "Did he not understand?"
  • Cross-L1 similarity in stages provides further evidence that developmental sequences are universal, not L1-specific.

6.2.4 Sociolinguistic Variation and Pragmatics

  • L2 learners' sociolinguistic patterns (regional, informal, age-appropriate speech) often diverge significantly from native speakers.
  • Example: French immersion students severely underused the informal "on" (for "we") vs native French Canadian speakers who use it almost exclusively.
  • More advanced learners show more native-like sociolinguistic choices (van Compernolle & Williams, 2009).
  • Pragmatics = how context and social situation determine language choices and their effects on interlocutors (speech (14), requests, politeness, forms of address).
  • Interlanguage Pragmatics (ILP) = the study of how L2 learners comprehend, produce, and develop pragmatic functions in the L2.
  • Pragmatic features vary greatly across cultures; transfer from L1 pragmatic norms can lead to miscommunication.
  • Some pragmatic distinctions are acquired late in both L1 and L2 (Kasper & Schmidt, 1996).
  • Five-stage developmental model for requests (Kasper & Rose, 2002):
  • Pre-basic: "Stamps please."
  • Formulaic: "I want stamps."
  • Unpacking formulas productively: "Can you give me stamps?"
  • More complex/indirect: "Could I have some stamps?"
  • Fine-tuning: "I would like to buy some stamps please."
  • EFL learners in classrooms detect grammatical errors more readily than pragmatic ones; ESL learners in naturalistic settings show the opposite pattern (Bardovi-Harlig & Dörnyei, 1998).

6.2.5 Reading

  • L2 reading involves both bottom-up (15) (letter/word recognition, syntax) and top-down processing (comprehension, semantic interpretation).
  • Word recognition is central: readers fixate on ~80% of content words and ~40% of function words.
  • Interdependence Hypothesis (Cummins, 1981): L1 reading proficiency transfers to L2 reading — strong L1 literacy supports L2 literacy development, provided a minimum L1 threshold is reached.
  • However, L2 proficiency (vocabulary, morphosyntax, listening comprehension) is equally or more important than L1 reading skill for L2 reading success (Alderson, 2000; Droop & Verhoeven, 2003).
  • Strong L2 vocabulary knowledge (≈8,000 word families for 98% text coverage) is particularly critical.
  • Script differences matter: moving between similar alphabetic systems (English–Spanish) is easier than between very different systems (English–Mandarin logographs).
  • Emerging consensus: both L1 reading skill AND L2 proficiency contribute to L2 reading, but L2 input exposure is the strongest overall influence (Koda, 2011).
  • Key principles for teaching L2 reading (Grabe, 2011) include: extensive practice, varied materials, pre/during/post-reading activities, building word recognition, vocabulary, comprehension strategies, reading fluency, and motivation.

6.3 Investigating Learner Language: Language Corpora

  • Language (16) = large computerized datasets of authentic learner language, enabling pattern and frequency analysis.
  • Key L2 corpora: ICLE (written), LINDSEI (spoken, advanced learners), French Learner Language Oral Corpora.
  • Corpora allow researchers to: identify common errors by proficiency/L1 group, trace formulaic language use, and re-examine developmental patterns longitudinally.
  • Corpus research generally confirms morpheme acquisition orders but also reveals significant individual variation (Housen, 2002).

6.4 Summing Up

L2 development is shaped by cross-linguistic (17), overgeneralization, markedness, developmental sequences, formulaic sequences, fossilization, and avoidance. These factors affect all linguistic sub-areas — phonology, vocabulary, grammar, sociolinguistics, pragmatics, and reading — in complex, interacting ways. Language corpora are increasingly valuable tools for investigating learner language systematically.

Chapter 7

The Age Factor

7.0 Chapter Overview

  • Age is one of the most debated topics in SLA research.
  • Widely held belief: the earlier one begins learning an L2, the greater the ultimate (1).
  • Common assumption: beyond a certain age, native-like proficiency cannot be achieved.
  • Chapter goal: examine what research reveals about the role of age and synthesize findings across studies.

7.1 The Critical Period Hypothesis (CPH)

Key Terms & Definitions

TermDefinition
Critical PeriodA window of time during which certain behaviors/skills are acquired with speed and ease, without external intervention
ImprintingRapid learning in a young animal that begins to model behavior on an adult; e.g., ducklings follow moving objects seen 9–21 hours after hatching
Critical Period Hypothesis (CPH)The view that there is a limited developmental period during which native-like L1 or L2 acquisition is possible; after which, ability declines
LateralizationThe process by which cognitive functions (especially language) become localized to one hemisphere of the brain (typically the left)

Historical Development of the CPH

  • Penfield & Roberts (1959): First proposed a critical period for language; set its end at ~9 years of age, citing "cognitive inflexibility" and loss of brain plasticity.
  • Lenneberg (1967): Brought major attention to CPH in Biological Foundations of Language; set onset at ~2 years and end at puberty (~13 years); noted that after puberty, foreign language learning requires "conscious and labored effort" and accents are difficult to overcome.
  • Birdsong (1999): Summarized CPH as stating that native-like acquisition is possible only within a limited developmental window; once passed, language-learning ability declines.
  • Long (1993): Clarified that CPH does not claim adults cannot reach high proficiency — only that they cannot reach native speaker levels.

Proposed Explanations for the CPH

  • Cerebral (2) (Lenneberg): Language functions lateralize to the left hemisphere; once complete (around puberty), flexibility is lost. However, Krashen (1973) argued lateralization is complete much earlier than puberty.
  • Genetic "use it or lose it" (Pinker, 1994): Language acquisition circuitry is dismantled after use, since maintaining it has a cost.
  • "Use it or keep it" (Bever, 1981): If the acquisition skill is not exercised by continuous L2 learning, it is lost; early L2 acquisition can delay the critical period.
  • Connectionist view (Elman et al., 1996): Learning itself creates an impediment to further learning — not due to a mechanism change but as a consequence of learning.
  • "Less is more" hypothesis (Newport, 1988): Children's limited cognitive capacity forces them to attend to small, meaningful linguistic units (morphemes, words), which benefits acquisition. Adults store larger, less precisely registered chunks.

Alternative to CPH

  • Speech Learning Model / SLM (Flege, 1995): L1 acquisition mechanisms remain intact in L2 learners; differences in L2 success are due to input quality/quantity and continued L1 exposure. Key finding: L1 phonological categories stabilize between ages 5–7, affecting new L2 category formation. Sounds very similar to an L1 sound are not perceived as new, leading to "merged" L1–L2 categories and non-native production.

7.2 Abnormal Instances: Children Raised in Isolation

Key Term

  • Feral (3): A child who grew up deprived of normal human interaction and language exposure.

Case Studies

  • Victor of Aveyron (1797, France): A ~12-year-old boy found after apparent abandonment; unable to learn to speak despite efforts. Confounds: possible autism, psychological/emotional trauma. Depicted in Truffaut's film The Wild Child.
  • Genie (1970, Los Angeles): Found at age 13 after being isolated and restrained from ~18 months to nearly 14 years. After rescue, she acquired some vocabulary and basic word order, but syntax and morphology remained severely limited (telegraphic speech: "eat cookie"). Confounds: psychological trauma and possible cognitive deficits make it impossible to attribute failure solely to missing the critical period. One speculation: left hemisphere atrophy prevented normal grammatical processing.
  • Significance: These cases offer partial support for the CPH but cannot be taken as definitive evidence due to complicating factors.

Curtiss (linguist working with Genie): Reflected that Genie's limited language still allowed her to encode concepts, share feelings, and interact with her world — and questioned whether grammar is ultimately what matters most for human connection.

7.3 Empirical Studies

Introductory Learner Profiles

  • Walid (heritage Arabic speaker, Detroit): Exposed to Arabic at home and English at school from early childhood; passes as a native speaker in both Arabic and English — illustrating the advantage of early bilingual exposure.
  • Mila's sons, Mirko & Dragan (immigrated from Eastern Europe in their teens): Retained native-like Bosnian; spoke English with a noticeable foreign accent even after years of US residence — unlike younger community members who arrived before their teens and passed as native English speakers.

Key Findings Across Linguistic Areas

  • Pronunciation/phonology: Most clearly affected by age of acquisition; widely accepted that post-childhood learners carry a detectable non-native accent.
  • Grammar/morphosyntax: Also affected, though with some variation across studies.
  • Vocabulary: Relatively immune to age-of-acquisition effects.
  • Different CPH age limits may apply to different linguistic domains.

Key Terms

  • Age of Arrival (AoA) / Age of Onset (AO): Age when a learner first receives substantial L2 exposure; AO is sometimes preferred as a more precise measure.
  • Length of Residence (LOR): Duration of time spent in the L2 environment.

Research Studies

Oyama (1976) — Pronunciation

  • Participants: 60 Italian immigrants in the US; AoA 6–20 years; LOR 5–18 years.
  • Tasks: Reading a short text and telling an anecdote; rated by native speaker judges.
  • Results: Earliest arrivals (AoA 6–10) performed like native speakers; those arriving after 12 had detectable accents. No effect for LOR.
  • Conclusion: Supports CPH for pronunciation.

Snow & Hoefnagel-Höhle (1978) — Multiple Linguistic Skills

  • Participants: 96 English L1 speakers learning Dutch in the Netherlands; three age groups: adults, adolescents (12–15), children (8–10).
  • Tasks: Pronunciation, morphology, imitation, translation; tested at 3, 6, and 9–10 months.
  • Results: Adolescents and adults outperformed children at 3 months; children caught up by 10 months.
  • Conclusion: Adults have an early speed advantage (partly due to greater metalinguistic awareness); children may match or surpass older learners in ultimate (4). Distinguishes speed of learning vs. ultimate attainment.

Flege, Munro & Mackay (1995) — Pronunciation

  • Participants: 240 Italian immigrants in Ontario (LOR 15+ years); 24 native English controls.
  • Task: Reading 5 simple English sentences; native evaluators judged presence of foreign accent.
  • Results: No sharp threshold; instead, a gradual deterioration in native-like ratings as AoA increased (see Figure 7.2).
  • Conclusion: Does not support a sudden "cut-off"; challenges strict CPH interpretation.
  • Follow-up (Flege, 2012): Re-recorded 160 original participants; ~⅓ had regressed in pronunciation, ~14% slightly improved. Greater English use with native speakers correlated with improvement. Coined: "you are what you eat, phonetically" — input quantity and quality may be the most important factor in L2 speech learning.

Bongaerts et al. (1997) — Pronunciation in Advanced Learners

  • Three studies; Dutch L1 learners of English (Study 2) and French (Study 3); AO 12+.
  • Tasks: Reading isolated sentences and frames tasks; rated by native speaker judges.
  • Results: Study 2 — 5/11 advanced learners rated as native speakers on some tasks. Study 3 — 3/9 passed as native-like across all tasks; some native speakers were rated as non-native.
  • Explanation for success: High motivation, intensive L2 input, and systematic training in L2 sound perception/production.
  • Conclusion: Offers evidence that CPH effects are not universal, though methodological limitations exist (isolated sentences may be easier than natural speech).

Patkowski (1980) — Grammar

  • Participants: 67 educated immigrants to the US (various L1s); AO 5–50 years; LOR 6–61 years; compared to 15 American-born controls.
  • Task: Native ESL teachers rated grammatical ability from 5-minute oral transcript samples (0–5 scale).
  • Results: Marked differences between pre-puberty (avg. AO 8.6) and post-puberty (avg. AO 27.1) groups; no effect for instruction or practice variables.
  • Conclusion: Supports an age effect (cutoff ~15 years) for native-like grammatical competence.

Johnson & Newport (1989) — Grammaticality Judgments

  • Participants: 46 native Chinese/Korean speakers at US universities; LOR 5+ years; 23 native English controls.
  • Task: 276-item grammaticality judgment test covering 12 morphosyntactic rules (listened and judged grammaticality).
  • Results: Strong correlation (−.87) between AoA and performance for arrivals aged 3–15; low correlation (−.16) for arrivals aged 17–39. Those arriving 3–7 performed like native speakers. Arriving at 20 gave no advantage over arriving at 30. Decline was gradual, not abrupt (see Figure 7.4).
  • Conclusion: Supports CPH, but suggests decline begins before 15 and continues gradually. Adults can still learn, but less efficiently with high individual variation.
  • Weaknesses: LOR may have been insufficient for some; some test items were too obviously ungrammatical, potentially inflating scores.

Birdsong & Molis (2001) — Replication of Johnson & Newport

  • Participants: 61 Spanish L1 learners of English; AoA 3–39 years; LOR 10+ years.
  • Results: No significant AoA correlation for arrivals under 16; marked effect for late learners. Decline continued across the lifespan (unlike Johnson & Newport's findings).
  • Conclusion: Challenges the idea of a well-defined critical window; the continuing decline counters strict CPH.

Abrahamsson & Hyltenstam (2009) — Comprehensive Proficiency Testing

  • Participants: 195 Spanish L1–Swedish L2 bilinguals.
  • Tasks: 10 cognitively demanding measures — speech production/perception, grammaticality judgments, formulaic language.
  • Results: No late learners (AO 13–19) passed as native-like on all measures; only 3 of 31 early learners (AO before 12) did so, despite 25/31 passing the initial global speech evaluation. No effect for LOR.
  • Coined term: "Non-perceivable non-nativeness" — subtle but real divergence from native norms, undetected in casual evaluation but revealed by demanding tests.
  • Conclusion: Most studies overestimate early learners' attainment due to insufficiently demanding tasks. The probability of a late learner achieving native-like command across all linguistic domains is "close to zero."

7.4 Exceptional Cases

  • Post-puberty learners who achieve very high L2 proficiency are rare but documented.

Julie (Ioup et al., 1994)

  • British woman; began learning Egyptian Arabic at age 21 (naturalistic, no formal instruction); examined after 26 years in Egypt.
  • Results: 7/13 native judges rated her speech as native-like; near-native on grammatical/translation tasks. However, ~half of judges did not consider her native-like, and she did not score 100% on morphosyntactic tasks.
  • Conclusion: Remarkable, but not irrefutable evidence against CPH; may reflect exceptional language (5) (discussed in Chapter 8).

Joseph Conrad

  • Native Polish speaker; learned English as an adult (after age 17), as a third language (after French); renowned English-language author of Heart of Darkness.
  • "The Joseph Conrad Phenomenon": Term used when an L2 learner achieves flawless morphosyntactic/lexical skill while retaining a native accent in speech.
  • Reality check: Conrad's speech was heavily Polish-accented (noted by Ford Madox Ford). His written English also showed non-native features (inverted adjective placement, misused prepositions, etc.) that required substantial editing by his editor, Edward Garnett.
  • Conrad himself wrote that English "remained a foreign language" requiring "formidable effort."
  • Conclusion: The "phenomenon" named after him is somewhat ironic — Conrad was skilled but not truly native-like in either speaking or writing.

7.5 Summing Up

  • Research strongly supports that L2 learners who begin after early childhood rarely achieve native-like pronunciation or morphosyntactic mastery.
  • Some post-puberty learners receive native speaker ratings on certain tasks — but more demanding tests typically reveal subtle differences.
  • There is no consensus on whether a strict critical "cut-off" exists or whether decline is gradual across the lifespan.
  • The appropriate benchmark of native-speaker-like ability is itself questioned: in bilinguals, two languages inevitably influence each other, so native monolingual norms may not be the most valid standard.

Chapter 8

Individual Differences

8.0 Chapter Overview

  • Individual difference (ID) factors studied in SLA include: intelligence, aptitude, attitudes, motivation, personality, learning style, cognitive style, and learning strategies.
  • Some IDs have strong links to L2 outcomes; others do not.
  • Key challenges: ID factors interact with each other; they are difficult to measure objectively (often rely on self-(1) or tests of disputed validity); definitions vary across researchers.

8.1 Intelligence

Key Terms & Definitions

TermDefinition
IntelligenceHypothesized general factor underlying the ability to adapt to the environment; often operationalized via IQ tests emphasizing academic skills
CALP (Cognitive/Academic Language Proficiency)Skills requiring abstract language abilities (analysis, synthesis) useful for academic tasks
BICS (Basic Interpersonal Communicative Skills)Skills useful for face-to-face personal communication and conversation

Views of Intelligence

  • Spearman's "g" factor: A single general ability underlying performance on all cognitive tasks; basis for traditional IQ testing.
  • Gardner's Multiple (2) (1983; 1999): Nine types — mathematical-logical, verbal-linguistic, musical-rhythmic, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal, visual-spatial, naturalist, and existential. Widely appealing in education. Critics (e.g., Sternberg) argue Gardner confuses "talent" with "intelligence."
  • Sternberg's Triarchic Theory (1985; 1988): Three components — componential (analytic thinking), experiential (creative thinking), and contextual (control over one's environment).

Key Study: Genesee (1976)

  • Participants: English L1 children (grades 4, 7, 11) in Quebec learning French; enrolled in immersion or traditional programs.
  • Tests: L2 reading, grammar, auditory comprehension, interpersonal communication, and IQ.
  • Results: IQ correlated with reading, grammar, and math — but not with auditory comprehension or interpersonal communication.
  • Interpretation (Cummins, 1979): IQ appears linked to CALP (academic skills) but not BICS (conversational skills). Traditional IQ may not predict everyday communicative L2 ability.

8.2 Language Learning Aptitude

Key Terms & Definitions

TermDefinition
AptitudeBasic abilities that facilitate L2 learning; controls rate of progress; varies between individuals
MLAT (Modern Language Aptitude Test)Carroll & Sapon's (1959) test measuring phonetic coding ability, grammatical sensitivity, and rote memory
Working Memory (WM)An active system for temporarily storing and manipulating information needed in complex cognitive tasks
Phonological Memory (PM)Subsystem of WM responsible for temporary storage of acoustic/speech-based information

Defining Aptitude (Carroll, 1981) — Four Criteria

  • Separate from general IQ and achievement
  • Separate from motivation
  • Stable, possibly innate
  • Not a prerequisite for SLA, but enhances rate and ease of learning

Learner Profile: Steve (Mandarin learner)

  • Demonstrated rapid progress in replicating native-like utterances — illustrating high phonetic coding ability and grammatical sensitivity.

The Modern Language Aptitude Test (MLAT)

Developed to help the US Army identify rapid language learners. Measures three components via five subtests:

SubtestComponent Measured
Part 1: Number learningPhonetic coding ability
Part 2: Phonetic scriptPhonetic coding ability
Part 3: Spelling cluesPhonetic coding ability
Part 4: Words in sentencesGrammatical sensitivity
Part 5: Paired associatesRote/associative memory
  • A fourth component — inductive language learning (inferring rules from input) — was identified by Carroll but not directly measured in the MLAT.
  • MLAT scores account for approximately 20–35% of variance in L2 achievement.
  • Skehan (1989; 1998) simplified aptitude to three core components: phonetic (3) ability, language analytic ability (combining grammatical sensitivity and inductive learning), and memory.

L1 Skills and Aptitude

  • Bristol Language Project (Wells, 1985; Skehan, 1986; 1989): Early L1 development (before age 5 — MLU, sentence complexity) correlated with language analytic aptitude scores in 13–14-year-old high school students.
  • Sparks et al. (2009): L1 literacy skills in elementary school predicted L2 aptitude and proficiency in high school.

Aptitude at Different Stages

  • Winke (2005): MLAT components predicted L2 learning at beginning levels but not advanced levels (Mandarin learners).
  • Harley & Hart (1997): Aptitude played a role at both early and late immersion levels (grade 1 vs. grade 7 start).
  • Robinson (2002): Traditional MLAT was validated for audiolingual classrooms; communicative classrooms may require aptitude measures emphasizing working memory for simultaneous attention to form and meaning.

Working Memory (WM) and Language Learning

  • WM = active mental workspace; limited total capacity; coordinates storage and processing of information.
  • Central executive: Coordinates attention and manages other WM components; measured by reading span tasks.
  • Studies (Harrington & Sawyer, 1992; Miyake & Friedman, 1998) link WM capacity to L2 proficiency.
  • Phonological memory (PM): Stores acoustic/speech information for a few seconds; tested via nonword repetition tasks.
  • PM linked to L2 vocabulary learning (Service, 1992; Cheung, 1996; French, 2006), grammar (Baddeley et al., 1998; French & O'Brien, 2008), oral fluency (O'Brien et al., 2007), and general L2 proficiency (Hummel, 2009).
  • PM effects may be strongest at beginning/intermediate levels; other factors may dominate for advanced learners.

Classroom Implication: Teachers can support learners with lower WM by providing written input alongside oral input, chunking tasks into shorter segments, and potentially using WM training programs.

Key Quote: "Research makes it clear that in the long run language aptitude is probably the single best predictor of achievement in an L2." (Gardner & MacIntyre, 1992, p. 215)

8.4 Personality

Learner Profiles

  • Xia Mei (Hong Kong immersion student): Extraverted, often first to volunteer; teachers linked her personality to rapid oral fluency gains.
  • Mila (Bosnian immigrant): Introverted, rarely volunteered in ESL class; attributed her slower fluency but stronger grammar/reading to her shy personality.

Personality Variables Studied in SLA Inhibition, risk-taking, anxiety, empathy, extraversion/(5).

Measurement

  • Myers-Briggs Type Indicator (MBTI): 16 personality types across four dimensions: Introversion/Extraversion, Intuition/Sensing, Thinking/Feeling, Judging/Perceiving.
  • Big Five (Costa & McCrae, 1985): Extraversion, Agreeableness, Conscientiousness, Neuroticism, Openness to experience.
  • Eysenck's Three Dimensions: Psychoticism (P), Extraversion (E), Neuroticism (N); proposed a biological/neurophysiological basis.

Eysenck's Arousal Theory and L2 Performance

  • Introverts: chronically overaroused nervous systems → interferes with STM (oral production) but benefits LTM (grammar, writing).
  • Extraverts: underaroused → full STM capacity → advantage in conversational/oral output; but may sacrifice accuracy for speed.
  • Dewaele & Furnham (1999): Confirmed extraverts are generally more fluent in both L1 and L2; possible speed/accuracy trade-off.

Other Research Findings

  • Strong (1983): "Talkativeness" in L1 (kindergarteners) linked to L2 oral proficiency.
  • Carrell, Prince, & Astika (1996): Introverts outperformed extraverts on vocabulary and course grades in EFL — possibly due to greater academic persistence.
  • Verhoeven & Vermeer (2002): "Openness to experience" and conscientiousness (Big Five) predicted L2 communicative competence.

Overall: Personality effects are real but complex; they interact with age, learning context, and the specific linguistic skill being measured.

8.5 Learning Style and Cognitive Style

Key Terms & Definitions

TermDefinition
Learning styleAn individual's natural, habitual, and preferred way of absorbing, processing, and retaining new information and skills
Cognitive styleA stable, internalized predisposition to process information in a characteristic manner
Field (6) (FI)Cognitive style: ability to perceive a part as distinct from its background; associated with analytic, rule-inferring ability
Field Dependence (FD)Cognitive style: tendency to perceive a field as a whole; associated with a holistic, interpersonal orientation

Key Distinction

  • Unlike abilities (which relate linearly to achievement), learning styles are bipolar — both poles offer advantages and disadvantages.
  • Cognitive style is more stable and biological; learning style is more changeable and potentially trainable.

Kolb's Experiential Learning Styles (1976; 1984) Four styles based on preferences for concrete experience (CE), abstract conceptualization (AC), active experimentation (AE), or reflective observation (RO):

StylePreference
DivergentCE + RO
AssimilativeRO + AC
ConvergentAC + AE
AccommodativeAE + CE
  • Willing (1988): Applied Kolb's model to adult migrants learning English in Australia; identified four styles: communicative, analytical, authority-oriented, and concrete.

Perceptual Learning Style Preferences (Reid, 1987)

  • Perceptual Learning Style Preference Questionnaire (PLSPQ): Identifies visual, auditive, kinesthetic, tactile, group, and individual preferences.
  • Reid (1987): Many non-English speakers favored kinesthetic/tactile styles; no relationship found with L2 proficiency.
  • Wang (1992): Found learning styles did correlate with EFL achievement in Chinese undergraduates.

Critique of Learning Style Research Ellis (2008): "Learning style" is ill-defined, overlaps with affective and cognitive ID variables, and not enough progress has been made to confidently link it to L2 outcomes.

Field Independence / Field Dependence (FI/FD)

  • Originated from WWII pilot disorientation research (Witkin et al., 1962).
  • FI: analytic, independent, able to separate figure from background — linked to grammar rule induction, interlanguage restructuring, formal rule learning.
  • FD: holistic, other-oriented — potentially better at interpersonal/communicative functions.
  • Measured by the EFT (Embedded Figures Test) or GEFT (group version).

Research Findings on FI

  • Genesee & Hamayan (1980): FI correlated with general French L2 proficiency and auditory comprehension, but not oral production.
  • Bialystok & Frohlich (1978): No significant correlations found.
  • Chapelle & Roberts (1986): FI correlated with L2 proficiency at both start and end of school term.

Critique of FI/FD Research

  • EFT may measure non-verbal intelligence, not cognitive style — raising validity questions.
  • Griffiths & Sheen (1992): Extrapolating from geometric shape perception to linguistic rule induction is unjustified — the construct is "remote and irrelevant" to language learning.
  • Witkin himself found little link between EFT and verbal learning.

Bilingualism

9.0 Chapter Overview

  • Expands the book's focus from sequential L2 learning to bilingual first language acquisition (BFLA): simultaneous (1) of two languages from birth or shortly after.
  • Key questions: How does BFLA compare to SLA? How do bilinguals develop, represent, and process two languages? Does bilingualism affect cognition?
  • Chapter covers: definitions of bilingualism, bilingual development (speech, vocabulary, grammar, mixed utterances), bilingual lexical representation and access, and cognitive effects.

9.2 Bilingual Development

Contextual Variability

  • Bilingual acquisition contexts vary enormously in: input quantity per language, functions of each language, who uses which language, community prestige, and stability of exposure.
  • Mackey's four dimensions — degree, function, alternation, interference — are all relevant to understanding a child's bilingual development.

Historical Debate: Is Bilingualism Harmful?

  • Early 20th century view: bilingual acquisition is detrimental to linguistic and cognitive development.
  • Saer (1923): Bilingual children (Wales, ages 7–14) scored 10 IQ points lower than monolingual English peers.
  • Saer, Smith & Hughes (1924): Suggested the mental ability difference persisted into university.
  • Critical flaw: Bilingual and monolingual groups were not matched for socioeconomic (3) (SES) — a key variable affecting IQ scores, making results uninterpretable.
  • Landmark study — Peal & Lambert (1962): Examined 10-year-old children attending French schools in Montreal; groups matched for SES. Result: bilinguals outperformed monolinguals on most IQ measures. Interpreted as evidence of greater mental flexibility in bilinguals. Limitation: single age group, single geographical/SES context — generalizability is limited. Still marked a turning point toward a positive view of bilingualism.

9.2.1 Early Bilingual Speech Perception and Production

Speech Perception

  • Recall from Chapter 2: infants initially discriminate sounds from all languages (universal perceptual system), but by 10–12 months, this narrows to the sounds of languages they are regularly exposed to (Werker & Tees, 1984).
  • Bilingual infants: Initially have a "unitary" system, then undergo perceptual (4).
  • Bosch & Sebastian-Galles (2003): Catalan-Spanish bilingual infants discriminated a Catalan vowel contrast (/e/ vs /ε/) at 4 months, lost it at 8 months, then regained it at 12 months. This pattern may reflect reorganization when similar phonological categories across two languages temporarily merge.

Speech Production

  • Bilingual children follow the same speech production stages as monolinguals (e.g., substituting fricatives with stops: "tay" for "say").
  • De Houwer (2009): Some bilingual children showed language-specific adult voicing patterns; others did not; individual variation is common.
  • Caramazza et al. (1973): Adult bilinguals show subtle differences in voicing onset — only detectable with specialized acoustic measurement, not by the average ear. Same may apply to bilingual children.
  • Abrahamsson & Hyltenstam (2009): Even among early bilinguals judged as native-like in global speech evaluation, only 3 individuals performed within native-speaker range on all detailed linguistic tasks.

9.2.2 Bilingual Vocabulary and Grammatical Development

  • Common belief: bilingual children develop language more slowly than monolinguals. However, De Houwer (2009) notes the wide normal range of monolingual development — bilingual children's development generally falls within this range.

Vocabulary

  • Input exposure has a strong effect on vocabulary size in both monolinguals and bilinguals (Hart & Risley, 1995; Pearson et al., 1997; Hoff et al., 2012).
  • Bilingual children rarely receive equal exposure to both languages → typically have a larger vocabulary in one.
  • Bilingual children have smaller vocabularies in each individual language than monolingual (5) (Marchman, Fernald & Hurtado, 2010).
  • However, when total vocabulary (words from both languages combined) is measured, bilingual and monolingual children are comparable (Hoff et al., 2012; Pearson, Fernández & Oller, 1993).
  • Junker & Stockman (2002): Bilingual toddlers' total vocabulary sometimes exceeded that of monolingual controls; up to 40% of bilinguals had a larger vocabulary in one language than the monolingual average.

Grammar

  • Vocabulary and grammar develop in a related fashion within each language but not necessarily across the bilingual's two languages (Conboy & Thal, 2006; Marchman et al., 2004; Parra, Hoff & Core, 2011).
  • Language-specific differences in development are driven by exposure differences.

9.2.3 Mixed Utterances

Key Term

TermDefinition
Mixed utterancesUse of words or morphemes from both of a bilingual child's languages within a single utterance (e.g., "Est-ce que we're going home?"; "I'm flatting the cat")

Learner Profile: Alberto's children

  • Occasionally mixed English words into Spanish and vice versa — similar to Alberto's own code-(6) behavior with fellow Latinos. Their overall skill in each language remained comparable to monolingual peers.

Earlier View (Volterra & Taeschner, 1978): Unitary System

  • Mixed utterances and failure to treat translation equivalents as distinct were interpreted as evidence that bilingual children start with a single merged linguistic system, later differentiating into two.

Current View: Early Differentiation

  • Research (Genesee, 1989; Meisel, 1989) shows children differentiate their languages from very early on.
  • Bilingual children as young as 1–2 years old generally produce words in the language of their interlocutor (Cantone, 2007; De Houwer, 2009).
  • Genesee, Boivin & Nicoladis (1996): Children as young as 2 years are sensitive to contextual cues about their interlocutor's dominant language.

Why Mixed Utterances Occur (Not Due to Confusion) Reasons include: adults in the environment code-switch frequently (Lanza, 1992; Quay, 2008); emphasis/highlighting (Goodz, 1989); a word or phrase is not readily available in one language; the concept does not exist in that language. Language switching is a productive strategy, not a sign of confusion.

CHILDES Transcript: Olivier (2;5)

  • Despite both parents using both languages freely during recording, Olivier produces virtually no mixed utterances — responding in the language of whoever addresses him. Demonstrates active, early language differentiation.

"One Person, One Language" Principle

  • Attributed to French linguist Maurice Grammont; advocated in Jules Ronjat's famous account of raising his son Louis in two languages (Ronjat, 1913).
  • Principle: each language should be represented by a different person, always consistently.
  • Evidence: Patterson (1999) found no difference in mixed utterance rates between children raised with one-parent-one-language and those in bilingual-parent households.
  • Goodz (1989): Even families claiming to follow the principle often do not strictly adhere to it.
  • Conclusion: Little empirical support for the principle as necessary to prevent language mixing.

9.3 Bilingual Lexical Representation and Lexical Access

Key Terms

TermDefinition
Bilingual lexical representationHow words from two languages are mentally stored or organized
Lexical accessHow words are accessed or recalled from the mental (7)
Language presentationThe way in which languages are used with a child (e.g., one language per parent)
Semantic primingExperimental task: deciding whether a letter string is a real word; faster responses when preceded by a semantically related word
Stroop experimentShowing color words printed in mismatching ink colors; participant must name ink color while ignoring the word

Weinreich's (1953) Three-Type Model of Bilingual Lexical Organization

TypeDescriptionExample
CoordinateSeparate concepts underlie each language's word"book" and "kniga" evoke distinct, language-specific associations
CompoundA single shared concept underlies both wordsBoth words evoke a general, undifferentiated concept of "book"
SubordinateL2 words are accessed via L1 equivalents"kniga" understood through the L1 word "book"
  • Associated with learning contexts: coordinate → separate contexts; compound → interchangeable use; subordinate → incomplete L2 learning.

Models of Bilingual Lexical Organization (Figure 9.2)

  • Single-store view (Kolers, 1968): Both languages share one unified mental lexicon.
  • Two-store view: Each language has a separate lexical store, connected at the conceptual level.

Revised Hierarchical Model (RHM) — Kroll & Stewart (1994)

  • Most widely discussed model; distinguishes the lexical level (word forms) from the conceptual level (meanings).
  • Strong links from L2 → L1 and from L1 → concepts (dominant language advantage).
  • Weaker links from L1 → L2 and from L2 → concepts.
  • Predicts: semantic context affects L1→L2 translation more than L2→L1 translation.
  • Counter-evidence (Heredia, 1997): both translation directions can be affected by semantic processing.
  • Revised interpretation (De Groot, 2011): translation always involves concept activation; asymmetries are due to differences in practice and processing ease, not structural differences.

Paradis's Three-Store Model (1979)

  • Proposes a single nonlinguistic conceptual level linked to two separate language-specific semantic/lexical stores.
  • Accounts for the fact that languages lexicalize concepts differently — e.g., English "ball" (any round bouncy object) vs. French "balle" (small) and "ballon" (large). Translation equivalents are often not perfect equivalents.
  • Captures the Italian saying: "Traduttore, traditore" — "To translate is to betray."

Modified Hierarchical Model (MHM) — Pavlenko (2009)

  • Improves on RHM by allowing conceptual distinctions.
  • Lexical concepts can be: shared (between languages), overlapping, or language-specific.
  • Language-specific concepts: e.g., Portuguese "saudade" (longing for a lost loved one) — has no single equivalent in English.
  • Lexical gaps can lead bilinguals to code-switch or borrow from the other language.

Bilingual Lexical Access

  • Key question: How does a bilingual instantly select the correct word from two full lexicons?
  • Cross-language activation: Multiple studies show bilinguals cannot switch off one language during processing — both remain active.
  • Semantic priming evidence: French-English bilinguals show faster responses to "hospital" when primed by the French word "infirmière" (nurse) than by an unrelated word (Perea, Dunabeitia & Carreiras, 2008). Neurolinguistic experiments confirm ongoing cross-language activation (Martin et al., 2009).
  • Note: Cross-language priming effects are generally weaker than within-language priming.
  • Stroop task evidence (Altarriba & Mathis, 1997): Bilinguals cannot ignore color words in one language when asked to name ink color in another — confirming both languages remain accessible simultaneously.

9.4 Bilingual Cognition

Key Term

TermDefinition
Executive controlBrain processes (localized in frontal lobe) associated with attentional control — focusing on a task in the context of competing or interfering information; the last part of the brain to mature; the first affected by aging

The Bilingual Cognitive Advantage

  • Hypothesis: bilinguals' constant management of two active languages trains attentional (8) — the executive control system.
  • Studies finding bilingual advantages on attentional control tasks:
  • Stroop tasks (Craik & Bialystok, 2006)
  • Switching tasks (Prior & MacWhinney, 2010)
  • Embedded figure tasks (Bialystok & Shapero, 2005)
  • Barac & Bialystok (2012): 104 six-year-old bilinguals (Chinese-, French-, and Spanish-English) outperformed monolinguals on executive control tasks.
  • Poulin-Dubois et al. (2011): Bilingual children as young as 2 years old outperformed monolinguals on a Stroop task.
  • Long-term effect: Some studies suggest bilingualism may delay onset of dementia symptoms (Craik, Bialystok & Freedman, 2010) — not slowing disease progression, but enhancing ability to compensate for cognitive decline.
  • Caveat: Not all studies confirm a bilingual advantage (e.g., Kousaie & Phillips, 2012).

Areas Where Bilinguals Are NOT Advantaged

  • Verbal fluency tasks: Bilinguals produce fewer words per category (e.g., naming animals) — possibly due to need to verify/manage two lexicons (Gollan, Montoya & Werner, 2002; Ransdell & Fischler, 1987).
  • "Tip of the tongue" experiences: Bilinguals report more frequent temporary word retrieval failures, particularly for low-frequency words (Gollan & Acenas, 2004).

9.5 Summing Up

  • Bilingual contexts vary enormously; generalization is difficult but research is clarifying common patterns.
  • Bilingual children develop similarly to monolinguals when total vocabulary across both languages is counted; early stages follow similar stages as monolinguals.
  • Bilinguals cannot "turn off" either language — cross-language activation is constant during lexical access.
  • The bilingual's two languages inevitably influence each other — monolingual norms may not be the most appropriate standard.
  • Growing evidence of cognitive advantages from bilingualism, particularly in executive/attentional control — countering earlier assumptions of cognitive disadvantage.
  • Cook's (1995) concept of "multi-(9)" encourages viewing bilinguals as unique individuals with multiple abilities, not as deficient monolinguals.
