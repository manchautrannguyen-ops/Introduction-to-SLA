Language learning in practice: the Audiolingual Method

During this period in which behaviorism was largely predominant, language educators attempted to develop teaching materials that reflected the CAH, including the belief that language learning meant establishing an entire new set of habits and breaking the former L1 habits. These views largely contributed to the development of the Audiolingual teaching method throughout North America. The Audiolingual Method (ALM) focused on establishing "good" language habits through repetition, mechanical language drills and pattern practices, often carried out through language laboratory exercises. The Audiolingual Method will be discussed further in Chapter 5.

Attempts to apply such a CA approach met with some success, but it soon became apparent that the approach was basically flawed. One problem that became evident quite rapidly is that it is very difficult, if not impossible, to make decisions about the degree of similarity or difference between languages because considerable A key weakness: assessments of L1–L2 similarity involved too much of this is involved. There was also a failure to find support for the predictions of CAH. For instance, one study (Whitman & Jackson, 1972) revealed that linguists' CA predictions applied to an English grammar test for Japanese native speakers were not supported: the Japanese speakers did not have most difficulty with the structures that differed the most from their L1. In addition, other studies (e.g., Hernandez-Chavez, 1977) revealed that learners often make errors in areas where the L1 and L2 are fundamentally similar. In fact, it may be the more subtle areas of difference between languages that cause the most problems.

There are other examples where cross-language similarity does not necessarily lead to The incorporation of features from one language into another is called ___, i.e., incorporating aspects of one language into another. For instance, researchers (Hulstijn & Marchena, 1989) found that Dutch L1 learners of English, whose L1 contains phrasal verbs (i.e., a verb plus a particle, as in "get up" or "give in") just as English does, tended to avoid using them and instead opted for a single word. In fact, they particularly avoided English items that most closely resembled their L1 phrasal verbs, i.e., "give up," "break out," "go off," and "bring up."

Given the lack of supporting evidence, researchers proposed a less categorical version of the CAH. Rather than stating that CA allows one to predict areas of difficulty for language learners, it was suggested that CA can help in explaining the occurrence of certain errors resulting from Errors resulting from the influence of the first language on the second into the L2. This more nuanced view of transfer across languages has come to be referred to as Abbreviated CLI; the broader modern term replacing L1 interference (CLI).

4.2

Error Analysis

The CA approach tried to predict and eliminate errors by emphasizing L1 interference. An examination of learner errors, however, ultimately led to the realization that many errors are not due to L1 interference, but rather to the learner's own contribution in attempting to master the new linguistic system. Learners make many errors that are due to Applying a rule too broadly (e.g., "I goed") is called ___ from their present understanding of the target language. For example, learners of English may tend to overgeneralize the past tense "–ed" ending on verbs: "I called" is correct, but "I goed" or "She hurted me" are not.

Another point that the CA approach did not take into account is the finding that as learners advance in their mastery of the target language, the type and frequency of errors changes. Errors made in later learning are not necessarily the same ones that tend to occur in the early stages.

In the 1960s, one researcher who came to be identified with a shift in thought about the role of errors in language learning was applied linguist Pit Corder. Corder argued that errors are important in providing a glimpse into the learning process and proposed a perspective that came to be known as Abbreviated EA; the systematic description and classification of learner errors (EA). Corder (1967, p. 167) pointed out: "A learner's errors . . . are significant in that they provide to the researcher evidence of how language is learned or acquired, what strategies or procedures the learner is employing in the discovery of the language."

An important development during this period was recognizing learner language as a true system with its own regularities, an Selinker's term for the learner's developing L2 system (abbreviated IL) (IL). Selinker (1972, p. 214) defined interlanguage as a "separate linguistic system based on observable output which results from a learner's attempted production of a TL [target language] norm." A more recent, but similar, definition is Bialystok and Sharwood Smith's (1985, p. 101): "Interlanguage: a systematic language performance (in production and recognition of utterances) by second language learners who have not achieved sufficient levels of analysis of linguistic knowledge or control of processing to be identified completely with native speakers." However, it was found that French speakers do not tend to make that error. Because of wrong predictions such as this (as well as growing interest in a very different linguistic perspective, Universal Grammar, which will be covered in Section 4.3), CA grew out of favor. Universal Grammar

As was pointed out in Chapter 2, a new view of language emerged during the 1950s that developed out of the structuralist school of thought, but which differed in important ways. Noam Chomsky, a linguist teaching at MIT, published a book (1957), Syntactic Structures, which proposed that languages are fundamentally similar and that a primary goal of the linguist is to describe the underlying rule system beneath the structure of all languages, known as Chomsky's term for the innate system of principles underlying all human languages (abbreviated UG). It was proposed that a set of universal principles characterizes all human languages. This view sharply contrasted with that held by behaviorists and structuralists in that it regarded language as an UG regards language not as environmentally shaped but as this — part of human genetic endowment, part of the human genetic endowment, and not as something that is largely crafted in response to environmental influences and pressures. Another term associated with the UG approach is "formalist" in that there is an emphasis on forms or structures, rather than behaviors, as characterized the behaviorist approach.

From a UG approach, a basic question is whether L2 learners continue to have access to UG constraints. As similarly argued in L1 acquisition, the "poverty of the stimulus" argument is raised, i.e., that exposure to linguistic evidence in the environment, the input, cannot on its own explain the abstract system of rules that ultimately characterizes the learner's language. In other words, learners appear to know what to do with a language even though they have not been explicitly taught certain rules; also they avoid making certain kinds of errors that are logically possible. How they do so is difficult to explain from looking only at the language they hear in their environment.

A considerable body of research has gone into investigating the relation between UG and second language acquisition. White (2003, p. 38) summarized the UG view in second language acquisition (SLA) as follows: ". . . claims for UG operation in L2 acquisition are simply claims that interlanguage grammars will fall within a limited range, that the 'hypothesis space,' is specified by UG." Language learning in practice: UG and language teaching

Chomsky himself suggested skepticism about any practical applications of UG theory to language teaching, as illustrated by these widely cited remarks: "I am frankly, rather sceptical about the significance, for the teaching of languages, of such insights and understanding as have been attained in linguistics and psychology . . . It is possible—even likely—that principles of psychology and linguistics and research in these disciplines, may supply insights useful to the language teacher. But these must be demonstrated, and cannot be presumed . . ." (Chomsky, 1966/71, pp. 152–155, cited in Widdowson, 2003.)

While UG theory may have led thus far to few direct applications to the classroom, its impact on theoretical aspects of second language acquisition has been major.

The Monitor Model / Input Hypothesis

In the mid-1970s, in the wake of the UG approach to language acquisition, applied linguist Stephen Krashen developed an influential model of L2 learning. Initially named the Krashen's original name for his model, based on the editing function of learned knowledge and, later on, the The later name for Krashen's model, emphasising the role of comprehensible input, the model consisted of five separate, though interconnected, hypotheses pertaining to second language learning. Much debate and interest has been generated from this model and it has left lasting traces in L2 classroom practices, in particular.

The first of the hypotheses was referred to as the Krashen's first hypothesis, distinguishing subconscious from conscious language development. This hypothesis states that there is a fundamental distinction between ways in which individual learners can develop language competence. One way is through Krashen called the process driven by this (not consciously focused on form) "acquisition" to language, in which the learner engages in communicative exchanges and is not consciously focused on language features, as tends to occur in naturalistic situations. Krashen labeled this process "acquisition." Another way is through conscious exposure to language data, as in a typical language classroom in which learners are taught the language through grammar and vocabulary exercises. This process was labeled "learning." In a controversial view in what became known as the " Krashen's controversial view that "learned" knowledge cannot become "acquired" knowledge," Krashen stipulated that what is "learned" cannot become "acquired" knowledge.

In this second hypothesis Krashen went on to state that "learned" knowledge served as a "monitor." According to Krashen (1982, p. 15): "Learning has only one function . . . as a Monitor or editor . . . Learning . . . makes changes in the form of our utterance, after it has been produced by the acquired system." The Monitor was stated to function like an editor, in that it could allow the learners to correct their utterances, under certain specific conditions: that the learner be focused on the One of three conditions for Monitor use: focus on this aspect of an utterance, that the learner have sufficient time to use the Monitor, and that the learner know the relevant grammatical rule necessary to correct the utterance.

The third hypothesis of the model was the Krashen's hypothesis claiming that language elements are acquired in a fixed, predetermined order. This hypothesis stipulated that language elements are acquired in an essentially fixed, pre-determined order. This position was largely based on a number of Research studies examining the sequence in which grammatical morphemes are acquired that had been carried out during this period, in which it was concluded that L2 learners from different L1 backgrounds tended to acquire L2 (primarily English) morphemes in the same sequence.

Language learning in practice: The Natural Approach

Krashen and Terrell (1983) applied the principles of the Monitor Model to the L2 classroom in the form of the Natural Approach, characterized by an emphasis on communicative activities, including exposure to large amounts of comprehensible input, or input slightly beyond students' current level. To reduce anxiety, students were encouraged not to speak until they were ready.

Specific classroom recommendations based on the Monitor Model (Dulay, Burt, & Krashen, 1982, pp. 263–269) were:

  1. Maximize students' exposure to natural communication.
  2. Incorporate a silent phase in your program.
  3. Use concrete referents to make input comprehensible.
  4. Devise techniques to relax students and protect their egos.
  5. Include time for formal grammar for adults.
  6. Learn motivations of your students and incorporate this into the lessons.
  7. Create an atmosphere in which students are not embarrassed by their errors.
  8. Use socially useful phrases in dialogues.
  9. Certain structures should be learned first. The plural, for example, will probably be acquired before the possessive in English.
  10. Do not refer to the L1 when teaching the L2.
4.5

Information Processing / Cognitive Approach

The Also called cognitive approach or cognitivism; applies mental processing concepts to SLA, also referred to more generally as a cognitive approach, or cognitivism, is a major perspective that largely developed out of the discipline of cognitive psychology in the 1950s. Applied to second language acquisition, this approach emphasizes that the mental processes used for interpreting experience are also involved in the acquisition and use of a second language. Therefore, while the UG approach specifies that there is a separate language faculty, the information processing approach assumes that the same mental faculties used for general problem solving and other cognitive activities underlie language use.

A basic premise of this approach is that the mind is limited in terms of its The mind is limited in this — a key premise of the information processing approach and this has important effects on the learning process. Mental processes occur in real time so that there are limits to what the mind is able to do at any given time. The SLA process is therefore viewed as affected in important ways by Three mental factors that the information processing view sees as constraining SLA.

One important distinction in this approach is between Slow, effortful, attention-demanding processing — characteristic of new skill learning and Rapid, effortless processing that takes over as a skill becomes practiced. Controlled processing characterizes new skill learning; it is relatively slow, requires attention, and is limited by short-term memory constraints. Over time, and through practice and training, a skill can come to be performed rapidly and with little effort, and automatic processing takes over. This increased automatization of processing skills results in increased fluency and greater comprehension on the part of the L2 learner.

Related to controlled and automatic processing are the terms Knowing that something is the case, e.g., knowing a grammar rule — contrasted with procedural knowledge and Knowing how to do something without conscious reflection — underlies automatic performance. Declarative knowledge consists of what the learner explicitly knows, as knowing the grammar rule that you need to add an "s" to a noun to form the plural in English. Procedural knowledge is knowing how to do something without having conscious awareness, as being able to produce L2 sentences without conscious reflection on what needs to be done first, second, etc., and is thought to underlie automatic performance. Traditional information processing views suggest that declarative knowledge precedes procedural knowledge.

4.6

Processing-Related Hypotheses

Several hypotheses related to the information processing/cognitivist perspective have been proposed which offer distinct views on the role of input and the learner's interaction with that input in second language acquisition. We will take a look at a few of these views in this section.

4.6.1 The Interaction Hypothesis

An influential view largely articulated by applied linguist Michael Long (1983; 1996), but which is related to similar views voiced by other scholars (Gass, 1997; Hatch, 1978), the Long's hypothesis emphasising the role of conversational interaction in making input comprehensible and promoting acquisition emphasizes the important role of conversational interaction in second language acquisition. This view suggests that the modifications occurring during interactions between conversational partners contribute to comprehension, in particular when The Interaction Hypothesis focuses especially on modifications that occur when this happens. While this perspective shares Krashen's emphasis on comprehensible input, it goes beyond arguing for simple exposure to input, by stressing the important role played by the learner's interaction with that input. Long argued that modified interaction is the necessary mechanism for making language comprehensible. The Interaction Hypothesis therefore proposes that interactional modifications make input comprehensible, ultimately enabling acquisition. Long (1983, p. 151) pointed out: "Native speakers modify not just their speech with non-native speakers, but various features of the interactional structures of their conversations with them."

Native speakers do in fact modify their speech in sustained conversation with non-native speakers. Although it is less used in current literature, the term " Ferguson's (1971) term for the simplified language native speakers use with non-native speakers" (Ferguson, 1971) has been used to refer to the modified language often used by native speakers in conversations with non-native speakers. Long's emphasis on modified interaction refers to both linguistic simplifications and conversational modifications. Some types of modified interactions include: elaboration, slower speech rate, gestures, contextual cues, comprehension checks, requests for clarification, Rephrasing a learner's incorrect utterance in correct form — e.g., "Oh, you bought a sandwich" (rephrasing an incorrect utterance correctly as when a learner says "I buy sandwich" and the instructor follows up with "Oh, you bought a sandwich"), self-repetition, and paraphrasing.

(Gass & Varonis, 1985, p. 51)

Long (1996) put particular emphasis on how modified interaction contributes to acquisition and underlined the importance of Long stressed this type of feedback during interaction as particularly important for acquisition during interaction. The phrase Long's term for the interactive process through which speakers adjust language until understanding is achieved (or negotiation of meaning) is used for what is considered to be the opportunity for language development. Long (1996, p. 418) defines negotiation for meaning as:

The process in which, in an effort to communicate, learners and competent speakers provide and interpret signals of their own and their interlocutor's perceived comprehension, thus provoking adjustments to linguistic form, conversational structure, message content, or all three, until an acceptable level of understanding is achieved.

Long (1996, p. 414) further explains how Long's term for drawing attention to a learner's incorrect utterances — facilitates acquisition, i.e., drawing attention in some way to learner's incorrect utterances, contributes to second language acquisition. As Long points out, the negative feedback that learners receive during negotiation helps draw attention to linguistic aspects not yet fully acquired by the learner. Learners benefit from interaction when they "notice the gap" (Schmidt & Frota, 1986) between the way they express their thoughts in the target language and the way a native speaker does so.

There is also evidence that L2 learners can benefit from conversational interaction with other L2 learners as well. In a study of conversational interaction between ESL learners (Long & Porter, 1985), 12 adult ESL learners—both intermediate and advanced—and six adult native English speakers served as participants. Learners were found to talk more with other learners than with native speakers. Furthermore, when the number of grammatical and vocabulary errors and false starts were compared, there was no significant difference between situations when learners were matched with other learners or with native speakers. The authors of this study concluded that learners themselves offer genuine communicative practice, including The interactive process of adjusting speech to achieve mutual understanding.

4.6.2 Comprehensible Output Hypothesis

The Swain's hypothesis that producing the L2 (not just receiving input) is essential to developing proficiency, originally influenced by cognitivist views, was primarily articulated by applied linguist Merrill Swain (e.g., 1985). This hypothesis suggests that having to communicate in the target language actually contributes to improvement: "producing the target language may be the trigger that forces the learner to pay attention to the means of expression needed in order to successfully convey his or her own intended meaning" (Swain, 1985, p. 249).

Swain looked at data from English L1 children enrolled in Swain studied L1 English children in these Canadian educational programs where instruction is delivered in the L2 in Canada who had little or no contact with the target language outside of school. She found that despite seven years of schooling, the Grade 6 immersion students differed notably from native speakers and that this was "particularly evident in those aspects of communicative performance which demand the use of grammatical knowledge" (Swain, 1985, p. 245). Swain argues that comprehensible input alone is not sufficient to ensure full L2 proficiency and suggests that what was missing from the immersion classrooms she studied were opportunities for Swain argued that opportunities to produce this were missing from immersion classrooms.

This hypothesis suggests that the demands of producing comprehensible output "push" learners ahead in their development. In contrast to Krashen's views described earlier in this chapter, which put all the emphasis on input, learner output is given an essential role and is thought to enhance fluency and lead to greater Greater fluency and reduced processing effort — a benefit of producing output. Another benefit is that producing output can help the learner analyze grammatical aspects of the target language, as the learner attempts to structure his or her thoughts. Output also serves to draw attention to Producing output helps learners notice these — areas where their L2 knowledge is incomplete: the challenge of having to put thoughts into concrete words and sentences helps the learner to realize where there is room for improvement in his or her L2 abilities. Another cited benefit of output is that it elicits relevant input.

4.6.3 The Noticing Hypothesis

The Noticing Hypothesis, formulated by applied linguist Richard Schmidt (e.g., Schmidt, 1990; 2001), was largely influenced by the information processing view of second language acquisition. An important distinction that underlies the Noticing Hypothesis is related to the differences among input, Schmidt's term for "that part of the input that the learner notices" — between input and output, and output. Input normally refers to the language to which the learner is exposed; as, for example, when the learner listens to the teacher in a classroom who is speaking in the target language. But input is not identical to intake. Since learners do not necessarily register all of the input to which they are exposed, a further distinction is necessary: intake is "that part of the input that the learner notices" (Schmidt, 1990, p. 139). There are many reasons why the input might not be noticed or registered: for example, lack of attention, incomprehension, and When processing demands exceed the learner's capacity — prevents noticing of input.

Schmidt formulated the Noticing Hypothesis based on the experience he had in following the L2 progress of "Wes" (a pseudonym), a Japanese native speaker and an artist, who moved to live in Hawaii, where the main language spoken is English. Schmidt observed Wes over a three-year period, leading to 18 hours of recorded oral transcripts (Schmidt, 1983; 1984).

Another important, but related, distinction is between L2 knowledge that underlies performance but of which the learner is not explicitly aware and Conscious knowledge of vocabulary, grammar rules, etc.. 4.6.4 Input Processing

Input processing (IP) is a view proposed by applied linguist Bill VanPatten (1996; 2004; 2007) which was also influenced by the information processing/cognitivist perspective. IP does not claim to be a model of language acquisition, but rather a model of how learners make VanPatten's IP is specifically a model of how learners make these during comprehension or, more precisely: "a model of what happens during comprehension that may subsequently affect or interact with other processes" (VanPatten, 2007, p. 115). VanPatten developed the IP model from certain basic assumptions:

  • Learners have limited processing capacity and cannot pay attention to form and meaning at the same time.
  • Learners tend to give priority to meaning. When the context in which they hear a sentence helps them make sense of it, they do not notice details of the language form.

IP suggests a number of ways in which learners go about processing the L2. For example, one important principle is known as the " VanPatten's IP principle stating that learners process lexical words before grammatical function words," and it proposes that learners process content words in the input before anything else. For example, in a sentence like "The cat is sleeping" the learner will first process the lexical content words "cat" and "sleep," and not the function words and inflections "the," "is" and "–ing."

A second important principle is "The First Noun Principle." This principle stipulates that learners are most likely to interpret the VanPatten's First Noun Principle: learners treat this element as the subject of a sentence in a sentence as the subject of that sentence. VanPatten points out (2007, p. 122) that this principle explains how it is that an English speaker will tend to misinterpret a Spanish sentence such as "Lo detesta Maria" (Maria hates him.) incorrectly as "He hates Maria" in which the pronoun "lo" is misinterpreted as the subject.

4.7

Emergentism and Usage-Based Approaches

As indicated in Chapter 2, another set of views that has grown out of the general discipline of cognitive science has been inspired by the scientific tradition known as The view that language phenomena are best explained by reference to more basic non-linguistic factors and their interaction. O'Grady (2010, p. 274) traces the roots of this tradition back to the nineteenth-century British philosopher John Stuart Mill's proposal that a system can have properties that amount to more than the sum of its parts.

In this perspective, learning emerges out of use or practice. With reference to language, emergentism proposes: "The phenomena of language are best explained by reference to more basic non-linguistic (i.e., 'non-grammatical') factors and their interaction—physiology, perception, processing, working memory, pragmatics, social interaction, properties of the input, the learning mechanisms, and so on." (O'Grady, 2008, p. 448).

Another term used in connection with a similar view about how learning occurs is The term for approaches holding that linguistic competence emerges from real communicative events and stored memories of utterances. By "usage-based" it is meant that: ". . . we learn constructions by engaging in communication. Thus an individual's creative linguistic competence emerges from the combination of two things: the memories of all the utterances encountered in communicative situations and the induction of regularities in those utterances based on A key factor in usage-based learning — the more often a pattern is encountered, the stronger its representation." (Ellis, 2007, p. 78).

4.7.2 Cognitive Linguistics

Another emergentist-inspired and usage-based approach that is becoming better known within the area of SLA is Abbreviated CL; sees meaning as central to language and language as inseparable from cognition. Cognitive linguistics (CL) as a distinct discipline is relatively recent, as the first journal devoted to its study, Cognitive Linguistics, appeared only in 1990.

This approach sees meaning as central to language, in contrast to the UG view that gives priority to the analysis of syntax. For the cognitive linguist, ". . . language, communication, and cognition . . . are mutually inextricable" (Ellis & Robinson, 2008, p. 3) and "language is a reflection of general CL argues language reflects these general mental processes — not a separate faculty" (Tyler, 2008, p. 459). Cognitive linguists exploring SLA therefore emphasize the importance of taking into account the learner's other cognitive abilities, and his or her interactions with the world. An emphasis is placed on the close links between CL places special emphasis on the close links between these two aspects of language, and the ways in which forms express meaning are given detailed examination.

Language learning in practice: Cognitive linguistics and language teaching

4.7.3 Language as a Complex Adaptive System

Another perspective within the emergentism framework is the view that language is fundamentally a Abbreviated CAS; language is acquired through combined influences of social interaction and cognitive processes (CAS) and is acquired and developed through the combined influences of social interaction and cognitive processes (e.g., Ellis & Larsen-Freeman, 2009). This view highlights the fact that language has, first and foremost, a social function: it allows us to communicate with each other. The CAS perspective proposes that our ". . . patterns of use strongly affect how language is acquired, is structured, is organized in cognition, and changes over time" (Becker, et al., 2009, p. 2) and that these ". . . processes of language acquisition, use, and change . . . are facets of the same system" (p. 2).

The Sociocultural Approach

Social constructivism is a theoretical viewpoint that emphasizes social interaction as the source for the construction of knowledge systems. A social constructivist approach to L2 learning, commonly identified as a sociocultural approach in the field of second language acquisition, or Abbreviated SCT; the view that social interaction is necessary for L2 development (SCT), has been articulated by some L2 researchers. Many of the concepts in this approach are based on the writings of Lev Vygotsky (1896–1934), a Russian psychologist and social constructivist. Vygotsky pointed out the fundamental role that social interaction plays in the general learning process. A central concept in the sociocultural approach is the notion of " Vygotsky's concept of how language serves as a "cultural tool" connecting us to others and the world." Vygotsky proposed that language is one of the basic means, or "cultural tools," by which we "mediate" or connect with others and with the physical world. Another important concept in this approach is " The process by which individuals interiorise knowledge acquired through social interactions," which refers to the process by which individuals interiorize knowledge acquired through their social interactions.

Another important Vygotskian concept is what he called the Abbreviated ZPD; the distance between what a learner can do alone vs. with expert help (ZPD):

ZPD: The distance between the actual developmental level as determined by independent problem solving and the level of potential development as determined through problem solving under adult guidance, or in collaboration with more capable peers. (Vygotsky, 1978, p. 86)

The ZPD is therefore the Vygotskian concept of the difference between what a learner can do without help and what he or she can do with help. In SLA, the definition of the ZPD has been reformulated as: "The distance between the actual developmental level as determined by individual linguistic production, and the level of potential development as determined through language produced In SCT, development occurs when language is produced ___, with a teacher or more proficient peer with a teacher or peer." Another important concept in SCT is The support structures provided by teachers or more capable peers to help learners reach the next stage. Scaffolding instruction refers to the "role of teachers and others in supporting the learner's development and providing support structures to get to that next stage or level" (Raymond, 2000, p. 176).
