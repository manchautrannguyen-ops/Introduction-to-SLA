5.1.9 Postmethod Perspective

In a relatively recent, critical view of previous methods in language teaching, the applied linguist Kumaravadivelu has argued that ". . . language teaching methods are based on idealized concepts geared towards idealized contexts" (Kumaravadivelu, 2012, p. 10). According to Kumaravadivelu (2012, p. 10): "We now know that there is no best method out there, that a futile search for one will only drive us to continually recycle and repackage the same old ideas and that nothing short of breaking the cycle can salvage the situation."

In his articulation of this Kumaravadivelu's critical view that no single method is universally appropriate, Kumaravadivelu (1994) points to increasing globalization, cross-cultural contacts, and changes in geopolitical forces as some of the factors making it necessary to offer a new vision of language teaching. In his view, teaching practices need to be adapted to each distinct The postmethod view argues practices must be adapted to each distinct ___ — not applied universally. He argues that teachers should not merely serve as "conduits" of previously determined knowledge about what and how to teach, but rather they need to take on role of " Kumaravadivelu's preferred teacher role — thinking deeply about principles, practices, and processes of instruction who deeply think about the principles, practices and processes of classroom instruction and bring to their task a considerable degree of creativity, artistry, and context sensitivity."

To replace the concept of "method" as the predominant operating principle in L2 teaching and teacher education, Kumaravadivelu proposes three parameters to act as operating principles: "particularity," "practicality," and "possibility." The first of Kumaravadivelu's three parameters — sensitivity to the specific context of language teaching refers to sensitivity to the particular context in which the language teaching takes place. The second parameter — breaks with tradition by emphasising that teachers are best placed to develop theory from classroom practice refers to the relationship between theory and practice, and breaks with tradition in emphasizing that teachers themselves are best placed to develop theory from their situated classroom practices. And the third operating principle "possibility" emerges from the notion of Kumaravadivelu's third parameter draws on this concept — the view that pedagogy can be used to maintain sociopolitical power, i.e., the view that pedagogy is used by the more powerful agents in a society (governments, corporations) as an instrument to maintain that power.

Grammar Translation Method A teaching method with an emphasis on grammar and translation of sentences.
Direct Method An approach developed in reaction to the Grammar Translation Method which gave priority to oral expression and avoided recourse to the learner's native language.
Audiolingual Method (ALM) Based on behaviorist principles, this method attempted to develop good language habits through repetitive drills and pattern practices.
Suggestopedia An L2 teaching approach developed by Georgi Lozanov that promoted a relaxed stress-free environment for language learning in small groups.
Total Physical Response (TPR) An approach developed by Asher that allows learners to wait until they are ready to speak, but asks them to carry out actions.
Communicative Language Teaching (CLT) An approach that emphasizes using techniques that engage learners in the pragmatic, authentic, functional use of language for meaningful purposes.
Task-based language teaching (TBLT) An approach related to communicative language teaching which emphasizes, through concrete learning outcomes in the form of "tasks," what learners are able to do with the language.
Postmethod perspective The view (e.g., Kumaravadivelu) that implementing any single method is misguided; teachers need to take their specific teaching context into account and take an active role.
focus on forms Placing an emphasis on formal aspects of language in the classroom by isolating them for practice through exercises, rather than drawing attention to forms through meaningful activities.
focus on form / form-focused instruction (FFI) Drawing students' attention to aspects of linguistic form in classrooms, characterized by a meaning- or communication-based approach.
input enhancement Various means of drawing attention to aspects of the target language, such as underlining or using bold type, so that students will notice and learn them.
input flooding Exposing learners to substantial amounts of input, or examples of a given linguistic feature.
Processing Instruction (PI) An approach to L2 teaching based on Input Processing which draws learners' attention to grammatical forms through various means.
corrective feedback Providing feedback to the learner, explicitly or implicitly, that an utterance is not entirely correct.
uptake Learner's utterance that follows and incorporates teacher feedback.
computer-assisted language learning (CALL) This refers to using the computer and computerized resources in L2 learning.
computer-mediated communication (CMC) Communication through electronic devices, such as sending emails, using text messages, video-based programs, etc.
